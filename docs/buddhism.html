Buddhism

Definitions

The Sangha is the monastic community of bhikkus (monks) and bhikkunis (nuns).

Yogācāra is an influential tradition of Buddhist philosophy and psychology emphasizing the study of cognition, perception, and consciousness through the interior lens of meditative and yogic practices.

A central tenant of Mādhyamaka (the middle most way) philosophy is that all dharmas are empty of svabhāva.

Svabhāva has been translated variously as “essence”, “intrinsic nature”, “inherent existence”, “own being” and “substance”.

Dukkha means “unsatisfactoriness”; it’s often translated as “suffering”.

Taṇhā means craving, desire or attachment; literally "thirst".

Saṃsāra means “world” as well as the cyclicality of all life, matter, and existence. Sometimes it’s also referred to with phrases like the “cycle of aimless drifting, wandering or mundane existence.” Its fundamental to most Indian religions.

Samadhi means a state of meditative consciousness. It is the last of the eight elements of the Noble Eightfold Path. It may refer to a broad range of states.

Cetanā, often translated “volition” or “intention”, is a mental factor that moves or urges the mind in a particular direction, toward a specific object or goal.

Saṅkhāra, often translated as “formations”, means “that which has been put together” and “that which puts together.” In the first passive sense, saṅkhāra refers to conditioned phenomena generally but specifically to all mental "dispositions". These are called 'volitional formations' both because they are formed as a result of volition and because they are causes for the arising of future volitional actions. In the second (active) sense of the word, saṅkhāra refers to karma that leads to conditioned arising, dependent origination.

People

Longchenpa (1308–1364) is a Tibetan scholar-yogi of the Nyingma school of Tibetan Buddhism.

Asaṅga (4th century CE) was one of the most important spiritual figures of the Mahayana Buddhism and the founder of the Yogācāra school.

Four Noble Truths

  1. Dukkha is an innate characteristic of existence in the realm of Saṃsāra
  2. Dukkha is “caused” by taṇhā
  3. Dukkha can be ended or contained by the renouncement or letting go of this taṇhā
  4. The noble eight-fold path is the path leading to the confinement of dukkha and taṇhā

The Noble Eightfold Path

  1. Right View
  2. Right Resolve
  3. Right Speech
  4. Right Conduct or Action
  5. Right Livelihood
  6. Right Effort
  7. Right Mindfulness
  8. Right samadhi

Observations

  • There is recursion in the Four Noble Truths in that the fourth truth is the eightfold path, and the first item is “Right View” which surely must include the Four Noble Truths.
  • Many Buddhists don’t think you should believe because of the authority of their texts, but rather because of direct observations you can make within your mind about the truth of what they say. However, if this is the case, I find it strange that Buddhism has split into so many different groups and sub-groups with different beliefs.
